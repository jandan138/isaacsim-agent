\begin{abstract}
We present a controlled systems study of embodied-agent execution in Isaac Sim
that isolates planner-to-executor contract design and lightweight runtime
validation. Across navigation and tabletop manipulation, we compare an
under-specified direct-action contract (P0), a typed tool-call contract (P1),
and the same typed contract with a brief self-check (P2), under bare dispatch
(R0) or validation with a single retry (R1). In the fixed-runtime
action-interface ablation, P0/R0 fails on all four evaluated tasks, whereas
P1/R0 and P2/R0 succeed on all four and eliminate invalid actions. In the
fixed-contract runtime-only ablation, validation plus one retry raises success
from 2/4 to 4/4 by converting invalid-first-action runs into successful
recoveries. Across the broader fixed task matrix, structured contracts remain
reliable across both task families, and P2 is associated with lower
planner/tool overhead than P1 in the covered comparisons. Because the
planner backend is deterministic by design, the reported comparisons are
descriptive over a fixed evaluation matrix rather than stochastic rollout
estimates. These results identify explicit action interfaces and lightweight
runtime validation as practical architectural design choices for embodied-agent
execution in the tested Isaac Sim setting.
\end{abstract}
